
\chapter{Invariant-Tree Algorithm}
\label{ch:hybridApproach}

This chapter contains the thesis-specific contribution: a detailed formulation of the invariant-tree algorithm and a corresponding termination analysis, based on the hybrid framework of \citep{MejstrikReif2025HybridApproachJSR}. In the recent literature, the same construction is also referred to as the \emph{tree-flavoured-invariant-polytope-algorithm}. Implementation details for finite $1$-bounding of generated product sets are taken from the cited work and are not claimed as original contributions here.

\section{Overview}
\subsection{Combination Strategies}
\label{sec:hybridStrategies}
At its core, the invariant-polytope algorithm constructs a polytope norm adapted to the matrix family, while the finite-tree algorithm controls growth through product decompositions. This naturally suggests combining both algorithms.

One option is modular: first run the invariant-polytope algorithm for several iterations, then use the resulting norm inside the finite-tree phase. A second option is concurrent: update norm information and tree-decomposition information in each iteration. Following \citep{MejstrikReif2025HybridApproachJSR}, we use the concurrent variant.

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:hybridProblemFormulation}
After preprocessing from Section~\ref{sec:introPreprocessing}, we obtain a scaled family $\tilde{\mathcal{A}}$. From the chosen candidate and the three-member inequality \eqref{eq:three_member}, we already know $\JSR(\tilde{\mathcal{A}}) \geq 1$. Hence proving $\JSR(\tilde{\mathcal{A}}) \leq 1$ is sufficient.
To simplify notation, we write $\mathcal{A}$ for this rescaled family throughout the remainder of the chapter.

As in Chapter~\ref{ch:finiteTree}, it is therefore enough to prove a polynomial product bound. More precisely, we show that there exist a polynomial $p$ and a norm $\lVert \cdot \rVert$ such that
\[
\lVert P \rVert \leq p(k) \quad \text{for all } P \in \mathcal{A}^k, \ k \in \N.
\]
The polynomial bound is obtained by decomposing each multi-index into finitely many parts arising from $1$-$v$-bounded test trees, plus one residual part controlled by finite-tree arguments.

\section{Algorithmic Formulation}
\label{sec:hybridAlgorithm}
The hybrid algorithm uses a vertex-wise version of the $1$-boundedness concept from Chapter~\ref{ch:finiteTree}. This is the local analogue of the $V$-closedness criterion from \citep[Definition 2.4 and Theorem 2.7]{MejstrikReif2025HybridApproachJSR}.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:oneVBoundedTree}
Let $v \in \R^d$ and let $\lVert \cdot \rVert$ be a vector norm on $\R^d$.
An $\AG$-tree $T$ is called \emph{$1$-$v$-bounded} with respect to $\lVert \cdot \rVert$ if
\[
\sup_{L \in \mathcal{L}(T)} \lVert Lv \rVert \leq 1.
\]
If
\[
\sup_{L \in \mathcal{L}(T)} \lVert Lv \rVert < 1,
\]
then $T$ is called \emph{strictly $1$-$v$-bounded}.
\end{definition}

If $\mathcal{P}=\mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V)$ is the polytope generated by the final vertex set, then, by Remark~\ref{rem:balancedPolytopeInducedNorm},
\[
\lVert Lv \rVert_{\mathcal{P}} \leq 1
\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad
Lv \in \mathcal{P}.
\]
Hence a family of trees $(T_v)_{v \in V}$ that is $1$-$v$-bounded for every $v \in V$ is the vertex-wise counterpart of a $V$-closed tree in the sense of \citep{MejstrikReif2025HybridApproachJSR}.

The algorithm starts, as in the invariant-polytope method, from a set $V_0$ of starting vertices.
Each vertex set $V_i$ defines a vector polytope norm $\lVert \cdot \rVert_{V_i}$.
We denote by $R_i \subseteq V_i$ the set of vertices that still have to be tested in iteration $i$.
For every vertex $v \in R_i$, we construct a test tree $T_v$.
If some $L$ in the leafage of $T_v$ satisfies $\lVert Lv \rVert_{V_i} > 1$, equivalently $Lv \notin \mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V_i)$, then $Lv$ is added to the next vertex set.
The next test set $R_{i+1}$ consists exactly of the vertices added in iteration $i$.
If $R_i = \emptyset$, then the algorithm terminates.
This is the exact closedness test from \citep[Algorithm~3.1]{MejstrikReif2025HybridApproachJSR}. The proof of termination only needs the weaker conclusion $\lVert Lv \rVert_{V} \le 1$ for the final polytope.

\subsection{Pseudocode}
Algorithm~\ref{alg:hybridApproach} is adapted from \citep[Algorithm 3.1]{MejstrikReif2025HybridApproachJSR}.

\begin{algorithm}
  \caption{Invariant-tree algorithm}
  \label{alg:hybridApproach}
  \begin{algorithmic}
      \State \Input{$\mathcal{A}$ (rescaled family of matrices), $\mathcal{G}$ (set of generators), $V_0$ (set of starting vertices)}
      \State $R_0 \gets V_0$
      \State $i \gets 0$
      \While {$R_i \ne \emptyset$}
          \State $V_{i+1} \gets V_i$
          \For {$v \in R_i$}
              \State Construct a test tree $T_v$ according to the current tree-selection rule
              \State $V_{i+1} \xleftarrow{\cup} \{Lv : L \in \mathcal{L}(T_v) \text{ and } \lVert Lv \rVert_{V_i} > 1\}$
          \EndFor
          \State $R_{i+1} \gets V_{i+1} \setminus V_i$
          \State $i \gets i + 1$
      \EndWhile
      \State \Return $\mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V_i)$
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Termination Theory}
\label{sec:hybridTerminationResults}

\begin{theorem}
  \label{thm:hybridTermination}
  If Algorithm~\ref{alg:hybridApproach} terminates, then $\JSR(\mathcal{A}) \leq 1$.
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Proof Strategy}
\label{subsec:hybridProofOutline}
The following argument is the vertex-wise analogue of \citep[Theorem 2.7]{MejstrikReif2025HybridApproachJSR}. Suppose the algorithm terminates after $K$ iterations, and write
\[
V=\bigcup_{i=0}^{K} V_i.
\]
We equip this final vertex set with the associated polytope norm $\lVert \cdot \rVert_V$. The proof has two steps: first, we obtain a polynomial bound for $A_Iv$ with $v \in V$ by combining $1$-$v$-boundedness with prefix-leaf decomposition. Second, we pass from the vertices to arbitrary unit vectors in $\mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V)$ by using the balanced-polytope representation from Definition~\ref{def:balancedPolytope}.

\subsection{Auxiliary Termination Results}

\begin{lemma}
  \label{lem:hybridArbitraryProductBound}
  If Algorithm~\ref{alg:hybridApproach} terminates, there exists a monotone polynomial $p$ such that for every product $P \in \mathcal{A}^k$ and $v \in V$, one has
  \[
    \lVert Pv \rVert_{\mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V)} \le p(k).
  \]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If the algorithm terminates, then the set of vertices $V = \{v_1, \ldots, v_M\}$ is finite and for each $v \in V$ there exists a finite $\AG$-tree $T_v$ that is $1$-$v$-bounded with respect to $\lVert \cdot \rVert_{\mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V)}$.
For every leaf $K \in \mathcal{L}(T_v)$ and every positive multi-index $L$ encoded by $K$, one has
\[
\lVert A_L v \rVert_{\mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V)} \leq 1.
\]
Hence, by Definition~\ref{def:balancedPolytope}, there exist coefficients $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_M$ such that
\[
A_L v = \sum_{j=1}^{M} \lambda_j v_j
\quad \text{and} \quad
\sum_{j=1}^{M} |\lambda_j| \leq 1.
\]
Moreover, Lemma~\ref{lem:finitePolynomialBound} provides a monotone polynomial $p_v$ such that
\[
\lVert P v \rVert_{\mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V)} \leq p_v(k)
\]
for every product $P \in \mathcal{A}^k$ encoded by a node of $T_v$.
Set
\[
p := 1 + \sum_{v \in V} p_v.
\]

We prove by induction on the length of $I$ that
\[
\lVert A_I v \rVert_{\mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V)} \leq p(k)
\]
holds for every positive multi-index $I \in \mathcal{I}^k$ and every $v \in V$, and also for the empty index $I = []$.
For $I = []$, we have $A_{[]} = I_d$ and therefore $\lVert A_{[]} v \rVert_{\mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V)} = \lVert v \rVert_{\mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V)} \leq 1 \leq p(0)$.
Now let $k \geq 1$ and assume the claim has already been proved for all shorter products.
If $A_I$ is encoded by a node of $T_v$, then
\[
\lVert A_I v \rVert_{\mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V)} \leq p_v(k) \leq p(k).
\]
Assume therefore that $A_I$ is not encoded by a node of $T_v$.
By Lemma~\ref{lem:agTreePLDecomposition}, applied to the tree $T_v$, there exist positive multi-indices $P$ and $L$ such that
\[
A_I = A_P A_L,
\]
where $A_L$ is encoded by a leaf of $T_v$ and $|P| < k$.
Writing
\[
A_L v = \sum_{j=1}^{M} \lambda_j v_j
\quad \text{with} \quad
\sum_{j=1}^{M} |\lambda_j| \leq 1,
\]
we obtain
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
    \lVert A_I v \rVert_{\mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V)}
    &= \left\lVert A_P \sum_{j=1}^{M} \lambda_j v_j \right\rVert_{\mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V)} \\
    &\leq \sum_{j=1}^{M} |\lambda_j| \lVert A_P v_j \rVert_{\mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V)} \\
    &\leq p(|P|) \sum_{j=1}^{M} |\lambda_j| \\
    &\leq p(k).
  \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where the third line uses the induction hypothesis and the last line uses the monotonicity of $p$.
This proves the claim and hence the lemma.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Main Termination Result}

\begin{proof}
  Let $V = \bigcup_{i=0}^{K} V_i$ be the final vertex set produced by the algorithm, and set $\mathcal{P} := \mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V)$.
  By Lemma~\ref{lem:hybridArbitraryProductBound}, there exists a monotone polynomial $p$ such that
  \[
    \lVert A_I v_j \rVert_{\mathcal{P}} \leq p(k)
    \quad \text{for all } I \in \mathcal{I}^k \text{ and } v_j \in V.
  \]
  Now take an arbitrary $x \in \R^d$ with $\lVert x \rVert_{\mathcal{P}} = 1$.
  By Definition~\ref{def:balancedPolytope}, there exist coefficients $\mu_1, \ldots, \mu_M$ such that
  \[
    x = \mu_1 v_1 + \ldots + \mu_M v_M
    \quad \text{and} \quad
    \sum_{j=1}^{M} |\mu_j| \leq 1.
  \]
  Therefore,
  \[
    \lVert A_I x \rVert_{\mathcal{P}}
    \leq \sum_{j=1}^{M} |\mu_j| \lVert A_I v_j \rVert_{\mathcal{P}}
    \leq p(k) \sum_{j=1}^{M} |\mu_j|
    \leq p(k).
  \]
  Hence
  \[
    \lVert A_I \rVert_{\mathcal{P}} = \sup_{\lVert x \rVert_{\mathcal{P}} = 1} \lVert A_I x \rVert_{\mathcal{P}} \leq p(k).
  \]
  By Remark~\ref{rem:balancedPolytopeInducedNorm}, the induced matrix norm $\lVert \cdot \rVert_{\mathcal{P}}$ is submultiplicative.
  Consequently,
  \[
    \JSR(\mathcal{A})
    = \lim_{k \to \infty} \max_{P \in \mathcal{A}^k} \lVert P \rVert_{\mathcal{P}}^{1/k}
    \leq \lim_{k \to \infty} p(k)^{1/k}
    = 1.
  \]
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
  If each test tree $T_v$ consists only of the root and its positive children, then Algorithm~\ref{alg:hybridApproach} reduces to Algorithm~\ref{alg:invariantPolytope}. This is the simplest $V$-closed-tree configuration from \citep[Theorem 2.8]{MejstrikReif2025HybridApproachJSR}, so the invariant-tree algorithm genuinely extends the invariant-polytope viewpoint.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
  Assume Algorithm~\ref{alg:hybridApproach} terminates and there exists a single test tree $T$ such that $T_v=T$ for all $v \in V$. Then the hybrid argument collapses to the fixed-tree argument of Chapter~\ref{ch:finiteTree}. Repeated application of Lemma~\ref{lem:agTreePLDecomposition} yields a factorization
  \[
    A_I = A_{I_r}\cdots A_{I_1},
  \]
  where each factor $A_{I_j}$ with $j=1,\ldots,r-1$ is encoded by a leaf of $T$, and the last factor $A_{I_r}$ is encoded by a node of $T$. Thus the leaf factors are bounded by $1$, while the last factor is bounded polynomially by Lemma~\ref{lem:finitePolynomialBound}. In this special case, no vertex-wise recursion is needed.
\end{remark}


\section{Implementation Considerations}
\label{sec:hybridImplementation}

Following the smallest-tree heuristic proposed in \citep[Section 3.2 and Figure 3]{MejstrikReif2025HybridApproachJSR}, we use the following terminology.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:hybridMinimalTree}
  Fix a matrix family $\mathcal{A}$ and a generator $G$. Among all $\AG$-trees that have a generator branch labeled by $G$ at the root, contain a first covered node, and satisfy the structural conditions from Definition~\ref{def:agTree}, we call a tree \emph{minimal} if its leafage is minimal among trees of this form. A fixed choice of such a tree is denoted by $T_{(G,\mathcal{A})}$.
\end{definition}

In principle, the invariant-tree algorithm allows a different test tree for each vertex $v \in V$, as long as the chosen tree satisfies the $\AG$-tree conditions from Definition~\ref{def:agTree}.

In this thesis, we restrict the implementation to fixed minimal choices. For each vertex, this keeps the generator branch and the first covered-node effect while minimizing the leafage that has to be tested. It therefore reduces the number of checks of the form $\lVert Lv \rVert_{\mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V)}$ and serves as the default tree-selection policy.

Because generator branches encode families such as $\{G^r : r \in \N_0\}$, a naive implementation would require infinitely many tests. Following \citep[Section~3.3]{MejstrikReif2025HybridApproachJSR}, we split the generator dynamics into a leading-eigenspace part and a stable remainder and then estimate the stable remainder by means of an $\varepsilon$-pseudospectral radius. Concretely, for a matrix $\Delta$ and $\varepsilon>0$, the $\varepsilon$-pseudospectral radius is
\[
\rho_{\varepsilon}(\Delta)
:=
\sup\bigl\{|z| : \|(zI-\Delta)^{-1}\|>\varepsilon^{-1}\bigr\}.
\]
It yields bounds of the form
\[
\|\Delta^n\| \le \varepsilon^{-1}\rho_{\varepsilon}(\Delta)^{n+1},
\]
so one chooses $\varepsilon$ in such a way that $\rho_{\varepsilon}(\Delta)<1$. This turns the infinite generator branch into finitely many verifiable conditions for the current vertex.

The practical implementation also uses a maximal iteration counter $r_{\max}$, meaning that at most $r_{\max}$ outer iterations are performed. If no certificate is found before reaching this threshold, the algorithm stops and reports the current bounds for the original, unscaled family. We denote the current lower bound by $\underline{\rho}$ and the current upper bound by $\overline{\rho}$. Here $\underline{\rho}$ is obtained from the best candidate product found in preprocessing, while $\overline{\rho}$ denotes the best upper bound currently available from the right-hand side of the three-member inequality together with the current norm or tree data. Thus the output is an interval
\[
\underline{\rho}\ \le\ \JSR(\mathcal{A})\ \le\ \overline{\rho}.
\]
In practice, these bounds can already be accurate even when full termination is not achieved.

From a computational perspective, the dominant costs are tree construction and the leafage checks $\lVert Lv \rVert_{\mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V)}$. Restricting to minimal trees keeps the tested leafage small. Generator branches add the finite verification procedure from \citep[Section~3.3]{MejstrikReif2025HybridApproachJSR}. No global polynomial-time bound in $(n,d)$ is expected in full generality, which is consistent with the known hardness results for JSR computation \citep{TsitsiklisBlondel1997HardToComputeJSR,BlondelTsitsiklis2000NPHardMaxAlgebra}.

Finally, candidate updates from the preprocessing stage in Section~\ref{sec:introPreprocessing} can still be combined with invariant-tree iterations to improve robustness in practice.

\section{Remarks}
\label{sec:hybridRemarks}

\subsection{Polytope Invariance}
\label{subsec:hybridPolytopeInvariance}
After Algorithm~\ref{alg:hybridApproach} terminates, the polytope $\mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V)$ is, in general, not invariant under the full family $\mathcal{A}$. This is expected, since the invariant-tree algorithm enforces polynomial product bounds rather than one-step invariance under each matrix in $\mathcal{A}$.

The termination certificate is first vertex-wise. This is the local version of the $V$-closedness concept from \citep[Definition 2.4]{MejstrikReif2025HybridApproachJSR}. For every $v \in V$, the algorithm provides a test tree $T_v$ such that each leaf matrix in $\mathcal{L}(T_v)$ maps $v$ back into the polytope. Equivalently, with
\[
\Gamma_V := \{(v,L) : v \in V,\ L \in \mathcal{L}(T_v)\},
\]
one has
\[
(v,L) \in \Gamma_V
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
Lv \in \mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V).
\]

A stronger, uniform statement is obtained if one can construct a single $\AG$-tree $T_\star$ that bounds all vertices simultaneously, i.e.
\[
\lVert Lv \rVert_{\mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V)} \le 1
\quad \text{for all } v \in V \text{ and } L \in \mathcal{L}(T_\star).
\]
Then $T_\star$ induces leafage invariance on the whole polytope:
\[
x \in \mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V),\ L \in \mathcal{L}(T_\star)
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
Lx \in \mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V).
\]
Indeed, writing $x=\sum_{i=1}^M \alpha_i v_i$ with $\sum_{i=1}^M |\alpha_i|\le 1$ and $v_i \in V$, one gets
\[
Lx=\sum_{i=1}^M \alpha_i Lv_i,
\]
and each term $Lv_i$ belongs to $\mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V)$ by assumption. By convexity and symmetry of $\mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V)$, this implies $Lx \in \mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V)$.

The existence and efficient construction of such a global tree $T_\star$ from the family $(T_v)_{v\in V}$ is a separate algorithmic problem and remains part of future work.

This invariance interpretation, together with the intended play on words relative to the invariant-polytope algorithm, motivates the name \emph{invariant-tree algorithm}.
