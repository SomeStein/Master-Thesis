
\chapter{Introduction and Background}
\label{ch:introduction}

The \textit{joint spectral radius} (JSR) was introduced by G.-C. Rota and G. Strang in 1960 \citep{RotaStrang1960NoteJSR}. It describes the maximal exponential growth rate of long matrix products drawn from a finite family. Since then, the JSR has become a central quantity in the study of discrete linear dynamics.

The JSR appears in several areas of mathematics and engineering. Important examples include wavelet theory and subdivision, where it enters the analysis of refinement schemes \citep{DaubechiesLagarias1992TwoScaleII}, and control theory, where it characterizes the stability of switched linear systems \citep{Jungers2009JointSpectralRadius}. The computational difficulty of determining the JSR has also motivated the development of specialized algorithms, including the invariant-polytope algorithm \citep{GuglielmiProtasov2013ExactComputationJSR} and the finite-tree algorithm \citep{MoellerReif2014TreeBasedJSR}.

Despite substantial progress, exact JSR computation remains difficult. The space of admissible products grows exponentially with the product length, and several hardness results show that no general efficient procedure should be expected.

This thesis has two roles. Chapters~\ref{ch:invariantPolytope} and~\ref{ch:finiteTree} summarize established algorithms and their termination mechanisms from the literature. The original contribution of the thesis is Chapter~\ref{ch:hybridApproach}, which develops a detailed formulation of Algorithm~\ref{alg:hybridApproach} and its termination theory on the basis of \citep{MejstrikReif2025HybridApproachJSR}. Implementation details for finite $1$-bounding of generated product sets are adopted from the cited work and are not claimed as original here.

\section{Notation and Preliminaries}
\label{sec:introPreliminaries}

Unless stated otherwise, all matrices are real $d \times d$ matrices and all matrix norms are submultiplicative. We use the following notation conventions consistently across all chapters:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Matrix families and dimensions:
    \[
        \mathcal{A}=\{A_1,\dots,A_n\},\qquad
        \tilde{\mathcal{A}}=\{\tilde{A}_1,\dots,\tilde{A}_n\},\qquad
        d=\text{matrix dimension},\quad n=|\mathcal{A}|.
    \]
    \item Generator notation:
    \[
        \mathcal{G}=\{G_1,\dots,G_m\},\qquad m=|\mathcal{G}|.
    \]
    \item Positive indices and products:
    \[
        \mathcal{I}:=\{1,\dots,n\},\quad
        I=[i_1,\dots,i_k]\in\mathcal{I}^k,\quad
        A_I:=A_{i_k}\cdots A_{i_1},\quad
        \mathcal{A}^k:=\{A_I:I\in\mathcal{I}^k\}.
    \]
    \item Scalars and spectra: $\alpha$ denotes a scalar, $x$ a vector, $\lambda$ an eigenvalue, $\rho(\cdot)$ the spectral radius, and $\sigma(\cdot)$ a singular value.
    \item Polytope norms:
    \[
        \|\cdot\|_{\mathrm{co}_{\mathrm{s}}(V)}=\|\cdot\|_V.
    \]
\end{itemize}

\section{Motivation and Applications of the Joint Spectral Radius}
\label{sec:introMotivationJSR}

Let $\mathcal{A} = \{A_1, \cdots, A_n\}$ be a finite set of $d\times d$ matrices. 
Consider a discrete-time linear system generated by a matrix sequence from $\mathcal{A}$:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:switched_system}
x_{t+1} = B_t x_t, \quad x_0 \in \mathbb{R}^d,
\end{equation}
where $B_t \in \mathcal{A}$ for all $t \in \mathbb{N}$.

A key question is whether the system is \emph{uniformly asymptotically stable}, i.e., whether every trajectory $(x_t)$ converges to zero for all initial states and all matrix sequences $(B_t)_{t\ge 0}$ with $B_t \in \mathcal{A}$:
\[
\lim_{t \to \infty} x_t = 0.
\]
This stability question is answered by a generalization of the spectral radius for a single matrix, the so-called \emph{joint spectral radius (JSR)} of a matrix set $\mathcal{A}$ \citep{RotaStrang1960NoteJSR}.
\begin{definition}\citep{RotaStrang1960NoteJSR}
    \label{def:jsrDefinition}
    For a finite set of matrices $\mathcal{A} = \{A_1, \dots, A_n\}$, the JSR is defined by
    \begin{equation} \label{eq:jsr_def}
        \JSR(\mathcal{A}) := \lim_{k \to \infty} \max_{P \in \mathcal{A}^k}  \|P\|^{1/k}.
    \end{equation}
    Here $\|\cdot\|$ denotes an arbitrary matrix norm.
\end{definition}
This definition quantifies the maximal asymptotic growth rate of matrix products from $\mathcal{A}$. Theorem~\ref{thm:jsrWellDefined} below shows that the limit exists and does not depend on the chosen norm.

\subsection*{Illustrative Example}

Consider the matrix set:
\[
\mathcal{A} = \left\{ A_1 = 
\begin{bmatrix}
0.5 & 1 \\
0 & 0.5
\end{bmatrix}, \quad
A_2 =
\begin{bmatrix}
0.3 & 0 \\
1 & 0.3
\end{bmatrix}
\right\}.
\]
Each matrix individually has spectral radius less than $1$. However, their product
\[
A_1 A_2 =
\begin{bmatrix}
0.5 & 1 \\
0 & 0.5
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
0.3 & 0 \\
1 & 0.3
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
1.15 & 0.3 \\
0.5 & 0.15
\end{bmatrix}
\]
has spectral radius of approximately
\[
\rho(A_1 A_2) \approx 1.2825 > 1.
\]
This example shows that switching can amplify the effects of individual matrices, leading to divergent behavior even if all single matrices are contractive. Thus, the JSR can exceed the spectral radius of any individual matrix in the set.

The following results motivate the importance of bounding or approximating $\JSR(\mathcal{A})$ \citep{Jungers2009JointSpectralRadius}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item If $\mathcal{A}$ is irreducible, then the system~\eqref{eq:switched_system} is \emph{uniformly bounded}, meaning $\sup_t \|x_t\| < \infty$ for every $x_0 \in \R^d$, if and only if $\JSR(\mathcal{A}) \leq 1$.
    \item It is \emph{uniformly asymptotically stable} if and only if $\JSR(\mathcal{A}) < 1$. 
\end{itemize}
Understanding the joint spectral radius is therefore critical for analyzing the asymptotic behavior of switched linear systems and motivates reliable computational methods \citep{Jungers2009JointSpectralRadius}. Beyond control theory, the JSR also appears in approximation theory and subdivision theory, with foundational references including stationary subdivision and two-scale refinement equations \citep{CavarettaDahmenMicchelli1991StationarySubdivision,DaubechiesLagarias1992TwoScaleII,Maesumi2000JSRHolderWavelets}. Related spectral-growth ideas are used in coding-theoretic capacity problems, including codes that avoid specified differences \citep{MoisionOrlitskySiegel2001CodesAvoidDifferences,BlondelJungersProtasov2006CapacityCodes}, and in discrete mathematics and combinatorics \citep{Protasov2000PartitionFunctionAsymptotic,JungersProtasovBlondel2008IntegerMatrixGrowth,JungersProtasovBlondel2009OverlapFreeWordsSpectra}. These links motivate efficient computational methods, including the invariant-tree algorithm studied in this thesis \citep{MejstrikReif2025HybridApproachJSR}.

\section{Fundamental Theoretical Results}
\label{sec:introTheoreticalBackground}

\begin{lemma}[Fekete's Lemma]\label{lem:feketeLemma}
Let $(a_n)_{n\ge 1}$ be a sequence of real, nonnegative numbers such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:subadditive}
a_{m+n} \le a_m + a_n \qquad \text{for all } m,n\ge 1.
\end{equation}
Then the limit
\[
\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{a_n}{n}
\]
exists and is equal to $ \inf_{n\ge 1} \left(\frac{a_n}{n} \right)$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Set $b_n := a_n/n$ and let $\alpha := \inf_{n\ge 1} b_n$.
Fix $n\ge 1$ and write any $k\ge 1$ as $k = qn + r$ with integers $q\ge 0$ and
$0 \le r < n$. By subadditivity \eqref{eq:subadditive},
\[
a_k = a_{qn+r} \le q a_n + a_r.
\]
Dividing by $k$ gives
\[
\frac{a_k}{k} \le \frac{q a_n}{qn+r} + \frac{a_r}{qn+r}
\le \frac{a_n}{n} + \frac{a_r}{qn}.
\]
Taking $\limsup_{k\to\infty}$ (equivalently $q\to\infty$) yields
\[
\limsup_{k\to\infty}\frac{a_k}{k} \le \frac{a_n}{n}.
\]
Since $n$ was arbitrary, we obtain
\[
\limsup_{k\to\infty}\frac{a_k}{k} \le \inf_{n\ge 1}\frac{a_n}{n} = \alpha.
\]
On the other hand, by definition of infimum we always have
$\alpha \le a_k/k$ for all $k$, hence $\alpha \le \liminf_{k\to\infty} a_k/k$.
Therefore $\liminf = \limsup = \alpha$, so the limit exists and equals $\alpha$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}\citep{RotaStrang1960NoteJSR,Jungers2009JointSpectralRadius}
    \label{thm:jsrWellDefined}
    The JSR is well-defined and independent of the choice of the matrix norm.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For a fixed matrix norm $\|\cdot\|$, define
\[
M_k := \max_{P \in \mathcal{A}^k} \|P\|
\quad \text{for } k \in \N.
\]
By submultiplicativity, every product in $\mathcal{A}^{m+n}$ can be written as a product of one element of $\mathcal{A}^m$ and one element of $\mathcal{A}^n$, so
\[
M_{m+n} \le M_m M_n
\quad \text{for all } m,n \in \N.
\]
Hence the sequence $a_k := \log M_k$ is subadditive. Lemma~\ref{lem:feketeLemma} therefore implies that
\[
\lim_{k \to \infty} \frac{a_k}{k}
\]
exists, and thus so does
\[
\lim_{k \to \infty} M_k^{1/k}
= \lim_{k \to \infty} \max_{P \in \mathcal{A}^k} \|P\|^{1/k}.
\]

To prove independence of the norm, let $\|\cdot\|_1$ and $\|\cdot\|_2$ be two matrix norms on $\R^{d \times d}$. By norm equivalence in finite-dimensional vector spaces, there exist constants $c,C>0$ such that
\[
c \|P\|_1 \le \|P\|_2 \le C \|P\|_1
\quad \text{for all } P \in \R^{d \times d}.
\]
Therefore
\[
\max_{P \in \mathcal{A}^k} \|P\|_2^{1/k}
\le C^{1/k} \max_{P \in \mathcal{A}^k} \|P\|_1^{1/k}
\]
and
\[
\max_{P \in \mathcal{A}^k} \|P\|_1^{1/k}
\le c^{-1/k} \max_{P \in \mathcal{A}^k} \|P\|_2^{1/k}.
\]
Passing to the limit as $k \to \infty$ shows that both norms yield the same value.
\end{proof}

To facilitate the analysis of the algorithms and presentation of the main results, we first establish fundamental properties of the JSR. The statements collected in this section are standard background results. When a proof is short and used later, we include it. Otherwise, we cite the literature and use the result as a reference point for subsequent chapters.

\subsection*{Homogeneity of the Joint Spectral Radius}
\begin{proposition}\citep{Jungers2009JointSpectralRadius}
    \label{prop:jsrHomogeneity}
    The JSR is homogeneous, meaning for any set of matrices $\mathcal{A}$ and scalar $\alpha \in \R$ we have
    \begin{equation}
        \JSR(\alpha \mathcal{A}) = |\alpha| \JSR(\mathcal{A}).
    \end{equation}
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
    Let $\mathcal{A} = \{A_1, \dots, A_n\}$ and let $\alpha \in \R$. For every product $P = A_{i_k} \cdots A_{i_1} \in \mathcal{A}^k$, the corresponding product in $(\alpha \mathcal{A})^k$ is $\alpha^k P$. Hence
    \begin{align*}
        \JSR(\alpha \mathcal{A}) & = \lim_{k \to \infty} \max_{P \in \mathcal{A}^k} \|\alpha^k P\|^{1/k} \\
        & = |\alpha| \lim_{k \to \infty} \max_{P \in \mathcal{A}^k} \|P\|^{1/k} \\
        & = |\alpha| \JSR(\mathcal{A}) \\
        & \qedhere
    \end{align*}
\end{proof}

\subsection*{Berger--Wang Characterization and Three-Member Inequality}
\begin{proposition}\citep{BergerWang1992BoundedSemigroupsMatrices}
    \label{prop:bergerWangCharacterization}
    The $\JSR$ can equivalently be defined as
    $$
    \JSR(\mathcal{A}) = \limsup_{k \to \infty} \max_{P \in \mathcal{A}^k} \rho(P)^{1/k},
    $$
    if $\mathcal{A}$ is a finite (or compact) set.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}\citep{RotaStrang1960NoteJSR,Jungers2009JointSpectralRadius}\label{prop:threeMemberInequality}
Let $\|\cdot\|$ be a matrix norm. Then for every $k\in\mathbb{N}$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:three_member}
\max_{P\in\mathcal{A}^k}\rho(P)^{1/k}\ \le\ \JSR(\mathcal{A})\ \le\ \max_{P\in\mathcal{A}^k}\|P\|^{1/k}.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Fix $k\in\mathbb{N}$ and choose
\[
P_{\max}\in\operatorname*{arg\,max}_{P\in\mathcal{A}^k}\rho(P)^{1/k}.
\]
For the left-hand side we obtain, for any $r\in\mathbb{N}$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:left_bound_three_member}
\begin{aligned}
\max_{P\in\mathcal{A}^k}\rho(P)^{1/k}
&= \rho(P_{\max})^{1/k} = \rho(P_{\max})^{r/(kr)} \\
&= \rho(P_{\max}^r)^{1/(kr)} \le \max_{P\in\mathcal{A}^{kr}}\rho(P)^{1/(kr)} \\
&\le \limsup_{r\to\infty}\max_{P\in\mathcal{A}^{kr}}\rho(P)^{1/(kr)} \le \JSR(\mathcal{A}).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

For the right-hand side, set
\[
M_n := \max_{P\in\mathcal{A}^n}\|P\|\qquad (n\in\mathbb{N}).
\]
By submultiplicativity of the norm, every $Q\in\mathcal{A}^{m+n}$ can be written
as $Q=RS$ with $R\in\mathcal{A}^m$ and $S\in\mathcal{A}^n$, hence
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Mn_submult}
\begin{aligned}
\|Q\|
&\le \|R\|\,\|S\|
\le M_m M_n,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
and taking maxima over $Q\in\mathcal{A}^{m+n}$ yields
\begin{equation}\label{eq:M_subadd}
\begin{aligned}
M_{m+n}\le M_m M_n
\qquad\text{for all } m,n\in\mathbb{N}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Define the real sequence $a_n := \log M_n$. Then from \eqref{eq:M_subadd},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:an_subadd}
\begin{aligned}
a_{m+n}
=\log M_{m+n}
\le \log(M_m M_n)
=\log M_m + \log M_n
=a_m+a_n,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
so $(a_n)_{n\ge 1}$ is subadditive. By Fekete's lemma, the limit
$\lim_{n\to\infty} a_n/n$ exists and equals $\inf_{n\ge 1} a_n/n$. In particular,
for the fixed $k$ we have
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fekete_step}
\begin{aligned}
\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{a_n}{n}
=\inf_{n\ge 1}\frac{a_n}{n}
\le \frac{a_k}{k}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Exponentiating \eqref{eq:fekete_step} gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rhs_bound_three_member}
\begin{aligned}
\lim_{n\to\infty} M_n^{1/n}
&= \exp\!\Big(\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{a_n}{n}\Big) \le \exp\!\Big(\frac{a_k}{k}\Big) \\
&= \exp\!\Big(\frac{\log M_k}{k}\Big) = M_k^{1/k} \\
&= M_k^{1/k} = \max_{P\in\mathcal{A}^k}\|P\|^{1/k}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Since $\JSR(\mathcal{A})=\lim_{n\to\infty}\max_{P\in\mathcal{A}^n}\|P\|^{1/n}
=\lim_{n\to\infty}M_n^{1/n}$ by definition, the right-hand inequality in
\eqref{eq:three_member} follows.
\end{proof}

This inequality underlies many computational methods, since it yields computable lower and upper bounds on $\JSR(\mathcal A)$. As $k\to\infty$, the upper bound $\max_{P\in\mathcal A^k}\|P\|^{1/k}$ converges to $\JSR(\mathcal A)$. The lower bounds $\max_{P\in\mathcal A^k}\rho(P)^{1/k}$ need not converge, but their limit superior equals $\JSR(\mathcal A)$ by Proposition~\ref{prop:bergerWangCharacterization}.

\subsection*{Similarity Invariance and Reducibility}

\begin{proposition}\citep{Jungers2009JointSpectralRadius}
    \label{prop:jsrSimilarityInvariance}
    The $\JSR$ is invariant under similarity transformations, i.e., for any invertible matrix $T$ we have
    \begin{equation}
       \JSR(\mathcal{A}) = \JSR(T^{-1}\mathcal{A}T).
    \end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
    By Proposition~\ref{prop:bergerWangCharacterization},
    \begin{align*}
    \JSR(T^{-1}\mathcal{A}T)
    & = \limsup_{k \to \infty} \max_{I \in \mathcal{I}^k} \rho(T^{-1}A_I T)^{1/k} \\
    & = \limsup_{k \to \infty} \max_{I \in \mathcal{I}^k} \rho(A_I)^{1/k} \\
    & = \JSR(\mathcal{A}),
    \end{align*}
    because the spectral radius is invariant under similarity transformations.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}\citep{Jungers2009JointSpectralRadius}
    \label{def:matrixSetReducibility}
    A set of matrices is called \emph{(commonly) reducible} if there exists a nontrivial subspace of $\R^d$ that is invariant under all matrices in the set. Equivalently, there exists a change of basis that block-triangularizes all matrices in $\mathcal{A}$ simultaneously. If $\mathcal{A}$ is not reducible, it is called \emph{irreducible}.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}\citep{Protasov1997GeneralizedSpectralRadiusGeometric}
    \label{prop:reducibleBlockDecomposition}
    If $\mathcal{A}$ is reducible, then 
    \begin{equation}
        \JSR(\mathcal{A}) = \max \{ \JSR(\mathcal{B}), \JSR(\mathcal{D}) \},
    \end{equation}
    where $\mathcal{B} = \{B_1,\dots, B_n\}$ and $\mathcal{D} = \{D_1,\dots, D_n\}$ are the blocks of the block-triangularized matrices
    \[
        T^{-1}A_jT = 
        \begin{bmatrix}
        B_j & C_j \\
        0   & D_j 
        \end{bmatrix}
    \]
    under some invertible matrix $T$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{example}
    \label{ex:reducibleMatricesBlockForm}
    \[
    A_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}, \quad
    A_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 2 \\ 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix}
    \]

    These matrices are reducible because there exists a nontrivial change of basis that transforms them into block-triangular form. The eigenvectors common to both matrices are

    \[
    v_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad
    v_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}.
    \]

    Using the change of basis matrix

    \[
    T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix},
    \]

    we compute the similarity transformations

    \[
    T^{-1} A_1 T = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad
    T^{-1} A_2 T = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}.
    \]

    Since both matrices are upper triangular in this basis, they share a common invariant subspace, proving that they are reducible.
    Interpreting the scalar blocks as one-dimensional matrix families, Proposition~\ref{prop:reducibleBlockDecomposition} yields
    \[ 
    \JSR(\mathcal{A}) = \max \{ \JSR(\{3,5\}), \JSR(\{1,1\})\} = 5
    \]
\end{example}

This decomposition can be applied iteratively until all resulting block families are irreducible. For the algorithmic chapters of the thesis, we may therefore restrict attention to irreducible families.

\subsection*{Finiteness Property and Spectrum-Maximizing Products}
\begin{definition}\citep{LagariasWang1995FinitenessConjecture}
    \label{def:finitenessProperty}
    A matrix set $\mathcal{A} = \{ A_1, \dots, A_n \}$ is said to possess the \emph{finiteness property} if there exists a finite sequence of matrices $A_{i_1}, \dots, A_{i_k}$ such that
    \begin{equation}
        \JSR(\mathcal{A}) = \rho(A_{i_k} \cdots A_{i_1})^{1/k}.
    \end{equation}
    Any such product is referred to as a \emph{spectrum-maximizing product} (s.m.p.).
\end{definition}
The finiteness property does not hold for all matrix families; counterexamples were constructed in \citep{BlondelTheysVladimirov2003CounterexampleFiniteness}. Nevertheless, spectrum-maximizing products remain central for practical algorithms, because many exact methods are built around promising candidate products.

\subsection*{Computational Complexity of JSR Computation}

The computation of the joint spectral radius (JSR) is theoretically challenging due to several complexity results.

Approximating the JSR is NP-hard \citep{TsitsiklisBlondel1997HardToComputeJSR}. Hence no polynomial-time algorithm is expected unless $\text{P} = \text{NP}$. Moreover, for general matrix families, even deciding whether the JSR is strictly less than one is undecidable \citep{BlondelTsitsiklis2000NPHardMaxAlgebra}. Despite these limitations, many structured instances still allow efficient numerical approximation.

\subsection*{S.m.p.-Candidates and Generators}
The algorithms discussed later start from products that appear to maximize the averaged spectral radius. In the invariant-polytope setting these serve as s.m.p.\ candidates, while in the finite-tree and invariant-tree settings they also give rise to generators. Identifying such products is therefore a key preprocessing step.

\subsection*{Spectral Gap}
\begin{definition}\citep[Definition 1.9]{MejstrikReif2025HybridApproachJSR}
\label{def:spectralGap}
    A finite matrix family $\mathcal{A}$ is said to possess a \emph{spectral gap} if there exists a constant $\gamma \in [0,1)$ such that for every $k \in \mathbb{N}$ and every product $A_I \in \mathcal{A}^k$,
    \[
    \rho(A_I) = \JSR(\mathcal{A})^k
    \quad \text{or} \quad
    \rho(A_I) \le \gamma\,\JSR(\mathcal{A})^k.
    \]
    Equivalently, every non-s.m.p.\ product is uniformly separated from the maximal growth rate by the factor $\gamma < 1$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}\citep{MejstrikReif2025HybridApproachJSR,GuglielmiProtasov2024SpectralGapFamilyMatrices}
\label{rem:spectralGapHybrid}
    In the invariant-polytope setting, a spectral gap is a key finite-termination assumption. It is used together with uniqueness, up to cyclic permutations and powers, and simplicity of the leading eigenvalue of the s.m.p.\ candidate. Later chapters show where this gap condition enters the algorithmic termination analysis. We also note that \citep{GuglielmiProtasov2024SpectralGapFamilyMatrices} studies a related notion of gap as separation between the first and second Lyapunov exponents for matrix families.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}
\label{ex:spectralGap}
    Consider
    \[
    A_1=\begin{bmatrix}2&0\\0&1\end{bmatrix},
    \qquad
    A_2=\begin{bmatrix}1&0\\0&2\end{bmatrix},
    \qquad
    \mathcal{A}=\{A_1,A_2\}.
    \]
    Since $\max\{\rho(A_1),\rho(A_2)\}=2$ and $\|A_1\|_\infty=\|A_2\|_\infty=2$, we get $\JSR(\mathcal{A})=2$.
    Any product $A_I \in \mathcal{A}^k$ has the form
    \[
    A_I = A_1^m A_2^{k-m}
        = \begin{bmatrix}2^m&0\\0&2^{k-m}\end{bmatrix},
    \]
    so
    \[
    \rho(A_I)=2^{\max\{m,k-m\}}.
    \]
    If $m=0$ or $m=k$, then $\rho(A_I)=2^k=\JSR(\mathcal{A})^k$ and $A_I$ is an s.m.p.\ product.
    If $1\le m\le k-1$, then
    \[
    \rho(A_I)\le 2^{k-1}=\tfrac12\,\JSR(\mathcal{A})^k.
    \]
    Hence Definition~\ref{def:spectralGap} holds with $\gamma=\tfrac12$.
\end{example}

\section{Preprocessing and Normalization}
\label{sec:introPreprocessing}
All three algorithmic chapters use the same preprocessing pipeline:

We want to find the JSR of the finite set of matrices $\mathcal{A} = \{A_1, \cdots, A_n\}$.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Assumptions}: $\mathcal{A}$ is irreducible. 
    \item \textbf{Candidates}: Find products $P = A_{i_k} \cdots A_{i_1}$ that maximize the averaged spectral radius
    \[
        \lambda := \rho(P)^{1/k}.
    \]
    \item \textbf{Rescaling}: Transform $\mathcal{A} \to \tilde{\mathcal{A}}$ with $\tilde{A_i} := \frac{1}{\lambda} A_i$.
    \item \textbf{Verification}: Prove $\JSR(\tilde{\mathcal{A}}) \le 1$. Together with the lower bound from the three-member inequality, this yields $\JSR(\tilde{\mathcal{A}}) = 1$. By homogeneity, this is equivalent to $\JSR(\mathcal{A}) = \lambda$.
\end{enumerate}

The algorithms considered in the thesis differ only in the verification step. The invariant-polytope algorithm seeks a vector norm whose induced matrix norm already bounds the matrices from $\mathcal{A}$. The finite-tree algorithm instead decomposes products into factors bounded by $1$ and one residual factor with only polynomial growth. The invariant-tree algorithm combines these two ideas.
